Endengered Species

Protecting Life Below Water empowering fishers and communities with data, maps and sustainable practices.

An endangered species is a type of organism, such as plants, animals, microbes, fungus, that is at risk of extinction due to a rapid decrease in its population or a loss of its habitat.

Why do species become endangered?

There are two main reasons:

  1. Habitat loss

    • Natural causes can lead to habitat loss. For example, dinosaurs became extinct because a sudden climate shift.
    • Human-driven factors like urban development, agriculture, and deforestation also destroy habitats. For instance, about 20% of the Amazon rainforest has been cleared in the last 50 years to create space for cattle ranches.
    • Invasive species introduced by humans can further threaten native species and ecosystems.
    • Loss of habitat can also lead to increased encounters between humans and wildlife. For example, whales may get entangled in fishing gear or struck by boats. Peoplle hunting or accidentally killing wildlife can also contribute to a species´s endangered status.

  2. Loss of genetic variation

    • Genetic diversity helps species adapt to environmental changes. However, nowadays, many species have low genetic variation due to small population sizes, inbreeding, and habitat fragmentation.
    • Human activity can also cause a loss of genetic variation. For example, overfishing has reduced the populations of many animals. The pressure on the fishing industry to catch more fish has led to a decrease in the number of healthy adult fish in the wild that can reproduce.

Characteristics of Endangered Species

Using international criteria, like the IUCN Red List, a species may be labeled endangered based on several factors. Currently, there are more than 169,000 species on The IUCN Red List, with more than 47,000 species threatened with extinction, including 44% of reef building corals, 37% of sharks and rays, 26% of mammals, 26% of freshwater fishes.

The Red List has nine levels of conservation. Seven of these levels rank how close a species is to extinction: least concern, near threatened, vulnerable, endangered, critically endangered, extinct in the wild, and extinct. The other two levels are data deficient, meaning that scientists do not have enough information to determine which category they belong. The other level is "not evaluated", which means that the species has not yet been assessed.

Classifying a species as endangered has to do with its range and habitat, as well as its actual population. For this reason, a species can be of least concern in one area and endangered in another. For example, the gray whale has a healthy population in the eastern Pacific Ocean along the coast of North and South America, however, in the western Pacific Ocean, the population is critically endangered.

Endangered Species in Mexico

Vaquita porpoise (Phocoena sinus)

The vaquita (Phocoena sinus) is the smallest cetacean in the world, whose name means “little cow.” Its most distinctive feature is a dark ring around its eyes, along with a proportionally large dorsal fin. The vaquita is unique among porpoises because it is the only species that lives in warm waters, and scientists believe its large dorsal fin helps release excess body heat.

This species can only be found in the northern part of the Gulf of California, Mexico. Vaquitas are shy and elusive, often avoiding boats. They are usually found in shallow waters up to 50 meters deep, where they feed on small fish, squid, and crustaceans.

Leatherback sea turtle (Dermochelys coriacea)

The leatherback turtle is the largest sea turtle species and the only surviving member of the Dermochelyidae family. Unlike other sea turtles, it does not have a hard shell but a leathery, flexible carapace with seven ridges along its back. This unique adaptation makes it one of the most distinctive marine turtles.

Leatherbacks are highly migratory, traveling across the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. In Mexico, they can be found in the Pacific coast, the Caribbean, and the Gulf of Mexico.

Elkhorn coral (Acropora palmata)

The elkhorn coral, named for its majestic antler-like branches, is a critically endangered reef-building species that plays a vital ecological role across the Caribbean, Gulf of Mexico, Florida, and the Bahamas. Once among the most abundant corals in its range, it has suffered steep declines due to disease, bleaching, storm damage, and human pressures.

This coral thrives in clear, shallow waters, often 1 to 5 meters deep, in high-energy zones with strong wave action. It flourished throughout the southern Florida coasts, the northern Gulf of Mexico, and throughout the Caribbean region, including areas off Mexico.

Endangered Species in United States

North Atlantic right whale (Eubalaena glacialis)

The North Atlantic right whale is one of the most endangered large whale species in the world. Fewer than 400 individuals remain, with only about 70 reproductive females.

Found along the eastern coast of the United States and Canada. They migrate seasonally: calving grounds in Georgia and Florida; feeding in New England and Canadian waters. Prefer shallow coastal waters and continental shelf areas.

Hawaiian monk seal (Neomonachus schauinslandi)

The Hawaiian monk seal is one of the rarest marine mammals, found only in the Hawaiian Islands. It is a species unique to the United States.

Lives in both the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands and the Main Hawaiian Islands. Uses sandy beaches for resting and pupping, and shallow reefs and lagoons for feeding.

West Indian manatee (Trichechus manatus)

The West Indian manatee (including the Florida manatee subspecies) is a gentle herbivorous marine mammal often called a "sea cow."

Found in shallow coastal areas, estuaries, rivers, and springs throughout the southeastern United States, especially Florida, and in the Caribbean. Depend on warm waters, especially springs and power plant outflows in winter, since they cannot tolerate cold stress.

Endangered Species in Spain

Angelshark (Squatina squatina)

The angelshark is a bottom-dwelling shark once common in European waters, now critically endangered. It has a flattened body and wide pectoral fins, making it look similar to a ray.

Once widespread across the eastern Atlantic and Mediterranean, now mostly restricted to areas like the Canary Islands, Spain.

Fin Whale (Balaenoptera physalus)

The fin whale is the second-largest animal on Earth after the blue whale. It has a sleek, streamlined body and is known for its speed and agility in the water.

Found in deep offshore waters of the North Atlantic, including the Bay of Biscay, Spain and Mediterranean.

Bottlenose Dolphin (Tursiops truncatus)

The bottlenose dolphin is one of the most well-known marine mammals, highly intelligent and social. It has a robust body, a short beak, and a curved dorsal fin.

It is found in coastal waters of Spain, including the Mediterranean Sea and Atlantic coasts.

Endangered Species in Japan

Japanese Eel (Anguilla japonica)

The Japanese eel is a catadromous species, this means that lives in freshwater but spawns in the ocean. It is an iconic part of Japanese cuisine, known as unagi.

It is found in rivers, lakes, and coastal waters throughout Japan, as well as in parts of China, Korea, and Taiwan. Spawns in the western Pacific Ocean, near the Mariana Islands.

Pacific Bluefin Tuna (Thunnus orientalis)

The Pacific bluefin tuna is a highly prized fish in Japan, especially for sushi and sashimi. It is known for its incredible speed and endurance, capable of swimming long distances across the Pacific Ocean.

Found in the western and central Pacific Ocean, including waters around Japan, Korea, and China.

Green Sea Turtle (Chelonia mydas)

The green sea turtle is a large marine reptile known for its herbivorous diet as an adult. It has a smooth, heart-shaped shell and is named for the green color of its body fat. It is culturally significant in Japan, often featured in folklore and art.

It is found in coastal waters around Japan, particularly in Okinawa and the southern islands.
